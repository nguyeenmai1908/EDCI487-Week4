Naming What We're Seeing
~9 minutes
↗ UN Resource

Greenwashing — making environmental claims that mislead more than they inform — is what the UN describes as promoting false solutions that distract from and delay real climate action.

How greenwashing works: 6 tactics to recognise

Using undefined words like "natural," "green," "eco-friendly," or "sustainable" that have no standard legal definition and can't be meaningfully challenged.

Example: "Our packaging is better for the Earth" — better than what? By how much? Verified by whom?

Claiming one product or practice is "green" while ignoring the company's broader environmental impact. The narrow claim is technically true; the full picture is not shared.

Example: A paper company advertising "sustainably sourced" paper while clear-cutting old-growth forests for other product lines.

Designing packaging with green imagery, leaf icons, or certification-style badges that suggest independent endorsement — without any actual third-party verification.

Example: A cleaning product with a "Certified Green" leaf symbol that was self-designed, awarded by no external body.

Making a claim that is technically accurate but entirely meaningless in context — often because the "achievement" is legally mandated or industry-standard.

Example: "CFC-free!" — CFCs have been banned since 1987. The claim is true and tells you nothing useful.

Promoting a product as "the sustainable choice" within a category that is inherently harmful — framing that obscures the larger harm of the category itself.

Example: "Greener" fossil fuels. "Eco-friendly" single-use plastics. "Responsibly sourced" fast fashion. The comparison is within a broken system.

Announcing ambitious climate pledges for 2050 with no credible near-term plan and no short-term targets — often while continuing to expand fossil fuel operations. The long timeline enables continued inaction today.

Example: An oil company pledging net-zero by 2050 while approving three new drilling sites in 2024, with no 2025 or 2030 targets published.
Knowledge Check
A clothing brand labels their new line: "Made Consciously — For a Better Tomorrow." Which tactic is this?
"Consciously" and "better tomorrow" are undefined. There's no standard they're held to, no third party verifying the claim. Pure vague language — the most common greenwashing tactic.
A fossil fuel company announces a net-zero pledge for 2050 but publishes no 2025 or 2030 targets. What is most concerning?
Exactly. The UN's expert group specifically flagged this: long-horizon promises with no near-term accountability allow companies to continue emitting while claiming climate leadership. The absence of 2025 and 2030 targets is the tell.
Reflect: Where might greenwashing appear in an educational context — materials, resources, or speakers that make it into K–12 classrooms?
Think about textbooks, guest speakers, corporate-sponsored curricula, school supply choices...
