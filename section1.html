Can You Spot It?
~4 minutes

Before we define anything, let's see what you already notice.

Below are three claims from companies and institutions. Read each one carefully. Which ones feel like genuine climate action — and which ones feel off to you?

These are fictional scenarios based on real documented cases. The tactics are real. Select your instinct, then reveal the analysis.

✈️
"FlyNorth Airlines is proud to be carbon neutral. Every ticket you purchase automatically offsets your flight's emissions through our certified reforestation programme in the Amazon."
– FlyNorth Airlines website, 2023
What's your read?
The net-zero mirage

"Certified" sounds reassuring – but certification standards for carbon offsets vary wildly, and reforestation projects in particular have been repeatedly found to overstate their impact. Investigations into similar real-world programmes found offsets overstating reductions by up to 90%. The emissions from each flight still entered the atmosphere. A confident claim with a vague credential behind it is the net-zero mirage in action – and it's one of the most common patterns in airline and corporate marketing.

🧵
"Don't buy this jacket." – A full-page newspaper ad from FieldWork Outdoor Co., alongside a breakdown of the exact environmental cost of producing one jacket: water used, emissions generated, waste created.
– FieldWork Outdoor Co. newspaper campaign
What's your read?
Skepticism needs evidence, not just instinct

This looks like a cynical stunt – a clothing company telling you not to buy things. But the ad was paired with a documented repair-and-resell programme, a published supply chain emissions audit, and independently verified targets to reduce production volume. Skepticism is healthy, but applying it equally to genuine efforts and bad ones means you can't tell them apart. The question isn't does this sound suspicious – it's what does the evidence actually show?

🏛️
"The Province of Westara has reduced its industrial carbon emissions by 30% since 2005 – proof that economic growth and environmental responsibility can go hand in hand."
– Westara provincial government press release, 2023
What's your read?
A rate or a total?

The 30% figure may be accurate – but it likely measures emissions intensity (emissions per unit of economic output), not total emissions. A region can cut its intensity while its absolute emissions rise, simply by growing its economy faster than its pollution. Governments and policy documents use intensity figures regularly because they tell a more favourable story. For teacher candidates, this matters beyond climate: knowing to ask "is this a rate or a total?" is a transferable critical literacy skill your students will use across subjects for the rest of their lives.
