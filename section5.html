Bringing It Into Your Classroom
~5 minutes

You'll be teaching in a world saturated with climate messaging — some genuine, much of it designed to create feelings of progress without requiring change. Your students will encounter this constantly: in advertisements, on social media, in educational resources, and sometimes in your own school's programming.

Critical media literacy around climate is not an optional add-on. It's a core competency for civic life in the 21st century — and it's something you can build into virtually any subject area or grade level.

Before you write — take a moment to think

Consider:

  • Where in a typical school day might greenwashing appear unquestioned?
  • What's one question you could ask students that would prompt critical thinking about a climate claim?
  • What would you need to feel confident teaching this content?
Reflection
Design one classroom activity that builds students' ability to critically evaluate climate claims.
  • The grade level and subject area
  • A brief description of what students do
  • One key question that anchors the critical thinking
  • How you would handle a situation where the activity surfaces corporate-sponsored materials already in use in your school

Think this through on your own — then bring your ideas to Week 4's discussion forum on Brightspace. Your thinking here will anchor your participation in this week's class conversation.

Seeing Clearly Is the First Act of Resistance

You've spent time with some of the most sophisticated tactics used to delay genuine climate action. Vague language. Impressive pledges. Moral cover for extraction. The antidote isn't cynicism — it's the critical, curious thinking you'll model for your students. The habit of asking: Who made this? What's left out? Who benefits? Who bears the cost?

Key takeaways from this module
01 Greenwashing delays real action by creating the impression of progress without the substance.
02 Six core tactics: vague claims, hidden trade-offs, false labels, irrelevance, lesser-of-two-evils, and the net-zero mirage.
03 Carbon colonialism describes how climate "solutions" can repeat colonial patterns of extraction and burden-shifting onto the Global South.
04 Critical media literacy — asking who made this, what's missing, and who benefits — is one of the most transferable tools you can give K–12 students.
