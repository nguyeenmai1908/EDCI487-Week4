You're the Decision-Maker
~10 minutes

Understanding greenwashing in theory is one thing. Recognizing it in the messy real world - where claims are confident and branding is professional - is harder.

In each scenario below, you're placed in a real decision moment. Read the situation, make your choice, and explore what the research says. All three reveals are worth reading - there's nuance in each path.

Scenario A The Supply Order
Situation

It's the first week of school. Your department head emails asking everyone to submit supply orders by end of day - the school gets a bulk discount if orders go in together. You need pencils for your Grade 7 class.

You find two options in the catalogue:

  • EcoWrite: "Made from sustainably harvested wood. Carbon offset certified." - $4.20 per pack
  • StandardMark: Plain label, no environmental claims - $2.80 per pack

Your school has a sustainability mandate. The department head is waiting. You have about ten minutes before you need to submit.

What do you order?
If you order EcoWrite

"Carbon offset certified" sounds reassuring - but certified by whom? Third-party standards like FSC or Gold Standard are meaningful; self-issued badges are not, and the catalogue doesn't say which this is. "Sustainably harvested" is a claim any company can make without independent verification.

Under time pressure, it's easy to let a green label do the thinking for you. That's exactly the environment greenwashing is designed for.

📌

The time pressure is real in schools - and it's worth noticing how it affects decisions. Slowing down enough to ask "certified by whom?" is a habit worth modelling.

If you order StandardMark

Reasonable - and EcoWrite's claims do deserve scrutiny. But blanket rejection of green-labelled products doesn't build the skill of distinguishing genuine certifications from marketing. The goal isn't permanent distrust; it's knowing what to check.

StandardMark's silence on sustainability also tells you nothing - absence of a claim isn't evidence of a better product.

The most critically informed move

You meet the deadline without endorsing an unverified claim, and you create a genuine classroom investigation: bring both packages in, look up EcoWrite's certification standard together, and ask students what they'd need to trust the label. The ten-minute time pressure was real - and that's worth naming too.

📌

"Who certified this, and what does that standard actually require?" takes five minutes online and turns a supply order into one of the most practical media literacy lessons you can run.

Scenario B The Assembly
Situation

It's October. Your school's Climate Action Week is coming up and the planning committee is struggling - three booked speakers cancelled and the budget is nearly gone.

A major energy company has offered a free, professionally produced "Climate Education Assembly" for the whole school. You've reviewed the presentation. It includes:

  • Legitimate statistics about the company's renewable energy investments
  • An engaging section on their "path to net zero by 2050"
  • No mention of their ongoing oil and gas operations, which represent 94% of their business

Your principal has already told the committee it looks great and is leaning toward confirming. You're a first-year teacher.

What do you do?
If you stay quiet

The statistics in the presentation may be accurate - but a picture that shows 6% of a company's activity as its identity is a partial picture presented as a complete one. Students who don't know the other 94% will leave with a shaped impression, not a full one.

Staying quiet protects your position but passes on exactly the kind of framing this module is about. It also misses the fact that you noticed - which means you already have the critical lens your students need.

If you recommend declining

A principled position - and worth raising, even as a first-year teacher. But declining leaves students without a supported opportunity to encounter and interrogate corporate climate messaging, which they'll face throughout their lives regardless.

It also puts you in an adversarial position with your principal over a resource that could become a teaching tool with relatively little adjustment.

The most pedagogically sound move

You raise the concern - which matters, even at cost to yourself - and offer a constructive path: run the assembly, then follow it immediately with a structured class discussion. Who made this presentation? What does the company's full business look like? What's the difference between a 2050 pledge and a 2025 target?

That sequence turns a potential problem into one of the most authentic media literacy lessons Climate Action Week could offer.

📌

Speaking up as a first-year teacher is genuinely hard. But naming what you noticed - constructively, with a solution attached - is exactly the professional posture worth building early.

Scenario C The Curriculum Unit
Situation

You're planning a Grade 10 Science unit on climate change. A colleague sends you a link to a free, beautifully designed unit called "Climate Solutions for the Future." You open it and find:

  • Genuinely strong inquiry-based activities - well-structured, student-centred, curriculum-aligned
  • A focus on technological solutions: carbon capture, hydrogen energy, renewables
  • No content on reducing consumption or systemic change
  • A footer on every page: "Developed with support from the NorthRock Mining Foundation"

Your colleague has already used it twice and loves it. Your department head has added it to the shared resource drive.

What do you do with this resource?
If you use it as-is

Your colleagues' approval and the strong pedagogy are both real. But industry-funded educational materials have a documented pattern: they emphasize technological solutions because those solutions don't require changing the industry's business model. A unit with no content on reducing consumption teaches students that consumption isn't part of the solution - which is itself a position.

Good pedagogy in service of a partial frame is still a partial frame.

If you don't use it

You'd be giving up genuinely strong classroom activities over a funding source - which is a harder case to make to your department head than it sounds. It also doesn't help students develop the ability to engage critically with industry-produced content, which is everywhere in climate communication.

The more durable move is teaching students to read the footer, not avoiding all content that has one.

The most nuanced move

Use what's strong. On day one, show students the footer and ask: what might a mining company want students to believe about climate solutions? Then run the unit - and add one session the unit doesn't include: what does reducing production and consumption look like as a climate strategy, and why might that perspective be absent here?

You keep the good pedagogy. You teach source literacy. And you give students the fuller picture the unit leaves out.

📌

"Read the footer" is one of the most transferable critical literacy habits you can build. Students who ask who funded a resource will use that question for the rest of their lives.
