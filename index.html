Green Washing

When performance replaces action

In 2023, Delta called itself the world's first carbon-neutral airline. H&M launched a "Conscious Collection." Innocent Drinks ran cartoon ads about fixing the planet — while owned by Coca-Cola, the world's largest plastic polluter. These weren't fringe claims. They came from some of the most recognized brands on Earth. And they were all found to be misleading.

Module Overview

Seeing through the performance

"The planet cannot afford delays, excuses, or more greenwashing."

— UN Secretary-General António Guterres, COP27

Before you arrive in class this week, this module will equip you to recognize greenwashing — not just in corporate advertising, but in the educational materials, sponsored resources, and school programming you'll encounter as a teacher.

You'll also explore carbon colonialism: the way climate "solutions" can repeat colonial patterns of extraction and burden-shifting, and what that means for justice-centred climate education.

Key Concepts

Three frameworks for this week

3 of 3 concepts
Concept 01
Greenwashing
Misleading the public into believing a company is doing more to protect the environment than it actually is — promoting false solutions that distract from and delay real climate action.
Concept 02
False Solutions
Climate strategies that appear credible but fail to reduce actual emissions — including poorly designed carbon offsets, vague net-zero pledges, and technological fixes that enable continued fossil fuel expansion.
Concept 03
Carbon Colonialism
The way wealthy, high-emitting nations shift the burden of climate action onto communities in the Global South — through carbon offset land grabs, emissions outsourcing, and imposed solutions without consent.
